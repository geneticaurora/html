1.4 Describe the risks that might impact digital projects – what causes digital projects too fail? Why?

Missing the point.

Misalignment with business processes: Marketing may build a beautiful new website with a great user experience, but if they overlook the resulting impacts on the company’s business processes, the initiative is very likely to fail. This often happens when teams put technology ahead of strategy, falling for the hype of new innovative tools before considering the business process implications of the new technology. Aligning digital and business goals and integrating new products seamlessly with business operations is critical to project success.

Skill shortage.

Lack of cross-functional expertise: Similar to the above, there's a common misconception that digital projects only need “digital people.” But every digital project should have someone with business process management skills to help scope and plan the alignment between the customer journey and back-end operational processes.

Focusing on the little things rather than the bigger picture.

Limited digital strategy. Many digital projects are focused solely on the objective of one department or initiative, rather than contributing to the overall company’s digital strategy and business goals. Define specifically what success looks like for the project and how it contributes to the company’s business strategy before moving forward.

Stiff firm and fragile.

Fixed plans that don’t account for change: The notion of a “5-year-plan” is dead. Using Agile, iterative processes that allow for frequent tweaks and improvement is the only way to ensure that success is lasting. Your digital project plans should be flexible enough to reflect business changes within the company and the market at any given moment.

Perfecting on Perfection.

Overambition: Expecting perfection instead of embracing the “done is better than perfect” mantra tends to result in projects that drag on way past their scope. Explore the idea of releasing “Minimum Viable Products,” or early versions of software that gauge how the market will respond and determine if the concept is worth pursuing. Saying "Let’s launch a perfect site within the next 3 months” without a real business plan is a sure path to failure.

Source: http://blog.celerity.com/5-reasons-digital-projects-fail-the-forgotten-role-of-business-process

Not listening.

Ideas are great, expectations are great, having a vision is great but if your asking the same question and getting the same answer and expecting different results this is where the project sings its swan song. A good analogy is Champagne ideas Lemonade budget.

Getting ahead of yourself.

Most organizations have more opportunities and project initiatives than they can ever hope to fulfill. Many companies embark upon more initiatives than they probably should, causing overworked and often unhappy team members and when this happens all enthusiasm sinks into the pit of CBA “can't be arsed”.

Vagueness

>Vagueness can lead us to assume, it can lead us into unknown territory that wastes time and effort, it can also create problems to the point of no return.

Before we can begin we need a brief, a comprehensive list of expectations and needs from the client or even ourselves so we can begin to explore what fits in which box. Let others be the guinea pigs

Research what other companies are doing, what are they using and what has been successful? also research on flexibility ,look around business tech models and industry specific business forums when researching as companies rarely report short cummings. Most important be nosy and get ahead no such thing as cheating when your providing the solutions; solutions take time and effort.

Choose a throne and where to place it.

It would be nice to stay within our comfort zone but that's not how solutions come alive. So we need to look at technology & language ecosystems that match the brief as closHe as possible also keeping within cost expectations. Do we choose cloud?, native?, cross platform? or hybrid? from the above points we can best judge that answer. I.e a small accountancy firm will not benefit fiscally from a bespoke native application; the time frame is too long and the cost is high and may become outdated quickly.

1.7 Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects – How does GDPR impact digital projects?

GDPR stands for the General Data Protection Regulation.

This regulation has been implemented in all local privacy laws across the entire EU and EEA region. It will apply to all companies selling too and storing personal information about citizens in Europe, including companies on other continents.

What GDPR means is that citizens of the EU and EEA now have greater control over their personal data and assurances that their information is being securely protected across Europe.

According to the GDPR directive, personal data is any information related to a person such as a name, a photo, an email address, bank details, updates on social networking websites, location details, medical information, or a computer IP address.

  1. right to access –this means that individuals have the right to request access to their personal data and to ask how their data is used by the company after it has been gathered.
  2. The company must provide a copy of the personal data, free of charge and in electronic format if requested.
  3. The right to be forgotten – if consumers are no longer customers, or if they withdraw their consent from a company to use their personal data, then they have the right to have their data deleted.
  4. The right to data portability – Individuals have a right to transfer their data from one service provider to another. And it must happen in a commonly used and machine readable format.
  5. The right to be informed – this covers any gathering of data by companies, and individuals must be informed before data is gathered.
  6. Consumers have to opt in for their data to be gathered, and consent must be freely given rather than implied.
  7. The right to have information corrected – this ensures that individuals can have their data updated if it is out of date or incomplete or incorrect.
  8. The right to restrict processing – Individuals can request that their data is not used for processing. Their record can remain in place, but not be used.
  9. The right to object – this includes the right of individuals to stop the processing of their data for direct marketing. There are no exemptions to this rule, and any processing must stop as soon as the request is received. In addition, this right must be made clear to individuals at the very start of any communication.
  10. The right to be notified – If there has been a data breach which compromises an individual’s personal data, the individual has a right to be informed within 72 hours of first having become aware of the breach.
